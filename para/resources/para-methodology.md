# PARA — Projects, Areas, Resources, Archive

By Tiago Forte. A universal system for organising digital information.

---

## Core principles

### Organise by actionability, not by subject

The foundational idea. Don't file things by topic (like school subjects) — file them by what they're *for*. The question is not "what is this about?" but "what outcome does this support?"

> "Instead of organizing information according to broad subjects like in school, I advise you to organize it according to the projects and goals you are committed to right now. This is what it means to 'organize by actionability.'"

### What's not active should be out of sight

Forte calls this the #1 principle of the entire book. Active work — projects with defined outcomes — sits in the foreground. Everything else is pushed to areas, resources, or archive, where it won't compete for attention.

This is the Cathedral Effect applied to digital spaces: a decluttered workspace produces clearer thinking, the same way physical architecture shapes cognition.

### The system must be simpler than the life it represents

> "If your organizational system is as complex as your life, then the demands of maintaining it will end up robbing you of the time and energy you need to live that life."

A map drawn 1:1 with its territory is useless. The value is in abstraction and simplification. If the system doesn't give back more time than it takes, it has failed.

> "PARA is the only one that stands the test of time because it gives you more time than it takes."

### Move quickly, touch lightly

Use intuition when organising. There is no single correct place for any piece of information — don't overthink the decision, just put it where it's most useful right now. PARA is dynamic; things move when you notice they fit better elsewhere.

## Three habits of organisation

Forte identifies three habits that underpin effective use of any organisational system.

### 1. Organise according to outcomes

Don't treat organisation as an end in itself. Organise only what helps move goals forward. Everything else can wait or be ignored.

### 2. Organise just-in-time

Organise as little as possible, as late as possible, and only as much as is needed. Pre-building elaborate folder structures is wasted effort — structure should emerge from actual use, not from planning.

This connects to the **desire paths** concept: don't lay sidewalks before anyone has walked. Let paths form naturally, then pave the ones people actually use. In practice: **never create an empty folder.** Only create structure when you have something to put in it.

### 3. Keep things informal

Only projects need precise definitions (a clear outcome and an endpoint). Everything else can be loose. Over-categorising silos information and prevents creative connections between ideas.

> "So much of what we call 'organizing' is essentially procrastination in disguise. We tell ourselves we're 'getting ready' or 'doing research,' pretending like it's progress."

## The four categories

PARA rests on a single observation: there are only four categories that encompass all the information in your life.

### Projects

Short-term efforts with a specific goal and a deadline or endpoint. A project has a defined outcome — when the outcome is achieved, it's done. Projects are "sprints."

**Examples:** complete website redesign, plan a holiday, finish an online course, set up a new computer, write a blog post, renovate the bathroom.

**Key test:** Can you imagine finishing this? If yes, it's a project.

**Sweet spot: 10-15 active projects.** Enough to pivot between when one is blocked. Few enough to see at a glance and review weekly. If you have 30, most aren't really active — archive or demote.

**False projects to watch for:**
- **Dreams without deadlines** — "learn Italian" or "get fit" have no defined endpoint. They're areas or someday/maybe items.
- **Hobbies** — crocheting, reading, playing guitar. Ongoing interests, not projects.
- **Megaprojects** — tasks so large they need breaking into multiple sub-projects. "Revamp the website" should become "rewrite the About page" and "redesign the UI" as separate projects.

**A project without a goal is a hobby, and a goal without a project is a dream.** If something lives in projects but has no clear done state, it's not really a project. If you have a goal but no project working toward it, nothing will happen.

**Red flag:** A "project" that's been running indefinitely with no end in sight is probably an area. Rename it and stop expecting it to finish.

### Areas

Long-term responsibilities that require ongoing attention but are never "done." Areas are maintained to a standard — they don't have an end state. Areas are "marathons."

**Examples:** health, finances, professional development, parenting, home maintenance, career.

**Key test:** Is this something you'll always need to manage? If yes, it's an area.

Every area should have a clear sense of "what does good look like here?" If you can't articulate the standard, the area is poorly defined.

### Resources

Topics you're interested in and learning about. The key distinction from areas: areas are *personally relevant responsibilities* (your health, your finances); resources are *generally useful interests and reference material* (recipes, gardening, a methodology guide).

> "The line between Areas and Resources is an opportunity to be completely honest with yourself: What is inside the circle of your responsibilities, which no one else is going to take care of for you, and what is outside?"

Resources should be **shareable** — they're not private to you. Areas are typically private. If you could hand it to someone else and it would be just as useful, it's a resource. If it only makes sense in the context of your life, it's an area.

**Examples:** science fiction, recipes, gardening, slide templates, methodology references, date ideas, music playlists, mental models, design assets.

**Key test:** Is this an interest or curiosity rather than a responsibility? If yes, it's a resource.

Resources are passive. Don't maintain them. Don't reorganise them. Save and forget until needed.

### Archive

Inactive items from the other three categories. Completed projects, areas you're no longer responsible for, resources that are no longer relevant.

**Key test:** Is this no longer active? If yes, archive it.

Archive liberally. An overstuffed active system is worse than an overstuffed archive. The archive is cold storage, not a graveyard — items can be retrieved when needed.

**Three levels of archive:**

1. **Project-level** — previous iterations kept alongside current work (e.g., earlier drafts within an active project).
2. **Area-level** — completed items within an ongoing area (e.g., finished blog posts within a "Writing" area).
3. **Top-level super archive** — related archived items grouped together (e.g., years of tax documents, all completed projects from a particular year).

**Information Bankruptcy:** When the system gets overwhelming, archive *everything* with today's date and start fresh. Previous items remain in cold storage, accessible if needed. This eliminates baggage and keeps the active system focused. It's a reset, not a loss.

## The critical distinction: projects vs areas

This is the #1 mistake people make with PARA.

**Projects are temporary. Areas are ongoing.** If you treat an area like a project, you'll feel like you're failing because it never finishes. If you treat a project like an area, you'll never make progress because there's no defined outcome to work toward.

Forte illustrates this with a coaching story. A Senior Director at a biotech firm, asked for a project list, produced items like "Strategic Planning," "Hiring," "Vacations" — all areas of responsibility, not projects.

Two problems result:

1. **You can't truly know the extent of your commitments.** "How much of a workload does 'Hiring' represent? It could be anything from a part-time hire every 6 months to filling 50 positions this quarter."

2. **You can't connect current efforts to long-term goals.** "Imagine the psychological effect of waking up week after week, month after month, and even year after year to the exact same list of never-ending responsibilities."

**How to tell them apart:**

- "Get healthy" is an area. "Complete a sleep study and get a diagnosis" is a project.
- "Manage finances" is an area. "File this year's tax return" is a project.
- "Maintain friendships" is an area. "Plan and book the Japan trip" is a project.

**The fix:** Break vague areas down into concrete projects with specific outcomes. "Hiring" becomes "Fill the marketing manager position." This creates "a cadence of regular victories" — the psychological reward of finishing things.

## Decision flowchart

When deciding where to put a piece of information, ask these questions in order:

1. **Will this help me move a current project or goal forward?** -> Yes -> **Project**
2. **Will this help me uphold an area of responsibility?** -> Yes -> **Area**
3. **Will it support one of my interests and curiosities?** -> Yes -> **Resource**
4. **None of the above?** -> Is it really worth keeping? Probably not.

## Moving between categories

| From               | To                                                                | When                                           |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| Area -> Project     | An area develops a specific outcome to achieve                    | "Health" spawns "Fix the sleep/airway problem" |
| Project -> Archive  | The outcome is achieved or abandoned                              | Project is done or no longer relevant          |
| Project -> Area     | The project outcome is achieved but ongoing maintenance is needed | "Set up the garden" becomes the "Garden" area  |
| Resource -> Project | Reference material becomes the basis for active work              | Research notes become a project plan           |
| Anything -> Archive | It's no longer active                                             | Move it, don't delete it                       |

Information's correct location depends on your *current relationship* to it. Move items between categories as that relationship changes. Options: move the file, move the folder, or link — but **never duplicate.** Every piece of information lives in exactly one place.

> "There is tremendous power in changing your organizational systems to fit your evolving needs and goals, instead of trying to force your needs and goals to fit your system."

## PARA and task management

PARA is an information organisation system, not a task manager. Forte is explicit about this — tasks and notes serve different cognitive purposes and belong in different tools.

### Tasks vs notes

**Tasks** are active, timely, convergent, and focused on execution. **Notes** are passive, timeless, divergent, and used for reference. Mixing them degrades both:

> "Your task list becomes less effective, doubling as a catalog of wishes, dreams, insights, reminders and surprises from your day."

A note might *generate* tasks, but those tasks should be moved to the task manager, not managed within the note.

### Where things live

| Information type       | Where it goes                        |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Appointments/meetings  | Calendar                             |
| Tasks and next actions | Task management app                  |
| Text, context, reference | Notes app (organised with PARA)    |
| Collaborative content  | Cloud storage                        |

### Connecting PARA to a task manager

Use the same project and area names across both systems. That's the link — not file references or URLs, just consistent naming so you can navigate between "what do I need to do" (task manager) and "why does this matter / what's the context" (PARA notes).

> "You do not need to perfectly sync your folders across all platforms. This would be a bad idea, because some platforms would have nothing in many of the folders."

The principle is consistency of naming and categories, not mechanical mirroring.

## Maintenance

### Five-minute weekly maintenance

A quick sweep to keep PARA current:

1. **Retitle** new inbox items — make generic titles descriptive
2. **Sort** items into PARA categories based on actionability
3. **Update** active projects — rename, check completion, split or archive as needed

Before archiving any project, move relevant items to areas or resources so nothing useful is buried.

### Multi-cadence reviews

Different parts of the system need attention at different frequencies:

- **Weekly** — review projects. What's active? What's stuck? What's done? (15-30 minutes)
- **Monthly** — review areas of responsibility. Are standards being met? Should anything become a project? Review someday/maybe items and reprioritise.
- **Annual** — the 50,000-foot view. Reassess life goals, update personal narrative, make conscious adjustments to direction.

Without regular reviews, small failures compound silently until catastrophic breakdown. The weekly review is "the backbone of everything else that happens during your week."

## Common mistakes

- **Organising as procrastination.** If you're spending more time perfecting the system than using it, stop and do the work.
- **Too many projects.** If you have 30, most aren't really active. 10-15 is the sweet spot.
- **No projects at all.** A list of only areas produces no sense of progress. Break areas into concrete projects with endpoints.
- **Areas without standards.** If you can't say what "good" looks like, you can't maintain the area.
- **Confusing areas and resources.** Be honest: is this your responsibility, or just an interest?
- **Resources as a dumping ground.** Resources should be genuinely useful reference material, not a guilt pile of things you saved and never read.
- **Never archiving.** The archive exists so the active system stays clean. Use it.
- **Duplicating information.** Every item lives in one place. Move, link, or tag — don't copy.
- **Pre-building structure.** Empty folders are speculative. Only create structure when you have something to put in it.
- **Treating PARA as a task manager.** Tasks belong in a task manager. Notes belong in PARA. Don't mix them.
